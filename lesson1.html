Part 1 · Understanding Feelings

Why We Feel

Feelings are signals, not commands.

A story-centered Islamic SEL lesson that teaches children how to notice a feeling, understand what it may be signaling, and choose a response guided by faith and wisdom.

Age10–13
Time25–30 min
SEL SkillSelf-awareness
Main ToolNotice — Name — Check — Choose

1. Main Story: Samir Is Left Out

Samir had been looking forward to the soccer game all morning. He had practiced passing the ball with his older brother during the week, and he hoped the other children would finally notice that he was improving.

At recess, two team captains stood in the middle of the field and began choosing players.

One name after another was called. Samir waited. He tried to stand tall and look confident. He told himself, "Maybe I will be next."

Then the final player was chosen.

Nobody chose Samir.

A few children ran toward the field without looking at him. One child laughed about something Samir could not hear.

Samir's stomach felt heavy. His face became warm. His throat tightened. He looked down so no one would see the tears forming in his eyes.

He remembered another game when he had been chosen last. A thought began growing inside him:

"They never want me. Maybe they all think I am useless."

Then another urge appeared:

"Say something cruel before they can embarrass you."

What is Samir probably feeling?

Samir's heart began beating faster. His shoulders became tight. A thought appeared:

"They must all hate me. I should say something mean before they laugh at me."

Which body signs show the feeling is becoming strong?

What might the feeling be signaling?

Samir's feeling was real. But the thought "everyone hates me" was not automatically a fact. And the urge to insult people was not a command he had to obey.

What should Samir do first?

Samir took a slow breath. He quietly said:

"I feel hurt and embarrassed. That does not mean everyone hates me. I can choose what to do next."

Which final response is wisest?

Samir paused and named the feeling: "I feel hurt, embarrassed, and left out."

He checked the thought "everyone hates me" and realized that he did not actually know why the captains made their choices.

He decided not to insult anyone or walk away forever.

After the game started, Samir spoke to the playground teacher and said calmly, "I was not chosen, and I feel left out. May I join the next game or help make the teams fair next time?"

The teacher listened. She explained that the captains had chosen quickly and had not noticed how the same children were being left out. She promised to organize the next round differently.

One of the captains later asked Samir to join a short passing game.

Samir was still hurt. Wise choices did not erase the feeling immediately. But he protected his words, his actions, and his dignity. He also gave other people a chance to understand and correct the situation.

2. Quran Anchor

وَأَنَّهُ هُوَ أَضْحَكَ وَأَبْكَىٰ

"And that it is He who makes one laugh and weep."

Surah An-Najm 53:43

Allah created human beings with the ability to laugh, cry, love, fear, hope, grieve, and become angry. Having a feeling does not automatically make a child bad.

Important balance: A feeling may tell us that something is happening inside us. But it does not automatically prove that every thought is true, and it does not decide what we should do.

3. Prophetic Lens

When the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ lost his young son Ibrahim, he cried. He did not pretend that sadness was absent. But he kept his words guided by Allah.

إِنَّ الْعَيْنَ تَدْمَعُ، وَالْقَلْبَ يَحْزَنُ، وَلَا نَقُولُ إِلَّا مَا يَرْضَى رَبُّنَا

"The eyes shed tears, the heart grieves, but we do not say except what pleases our Lord."

Sahih al-Bukhari 1303

What the Prophet ﷺ did

He allowed the sadness to be real. He cried and acknowledged that his heart was grieving.

What guided him

His feeling did not command harmful words. Faith guided what he chose to say.

Strong faith does not mean having no feelings. It means letting Allah's guidance lead our response.

4. Understand the Difference

Signal

"I feel left out. I want belonging and respect."

Unhelpful Thought or Urge

"Everyone hates me. I should hurt them back."

Wise Choice

Pause, check what is true, and choose words or actions that please Allah.

Do not teach: "Every feeling tells the truth." Feelings are real experiences, but the thoughts attached to them can sometimes be mistaken or exaggerated.

5. Notice — Name It to Tame It — Check — Choose

1

Notice

What is happening in my body?

· Hot face
· Tight chest
· Heavy stomach
· Tears
· Fast heartbeat
· Tight shoulders

2

Name It to Tame It

A strong feeling can feel like a storm with no name. When I name it, I understand it better. Naming the feeling does not make it disappear, but it can make it easier to manage.

Say: "I feel angry."
Not: "I am an angry person."

Before: "Everything is terrible!"
After: "I feel embarrassed because I was left out."
3

Check

· What may this feeling be signaling?
· What thought came with it?
· Is that thought certainly true?
· What is the feeling pushing me to do?
· Could there be another explanation?

4

Choose

What response is safe, fair, wise, and pleasing to Allah?

· Speak calmly
· Ask for help
· Wait before responding
· Move away from harm
· Correct a misunderstanding
· Apologize or repair if needed

I notice my feeling. I name it to tame it. I check the thought and urge. I choose what pleases Allah.

6. Transfer Practice: Layla's Presentation

Layla had practiced her science presentation at home three times. She knew the main ideas, but she still felt nervous about speaking in front of the class.

During the presentation, she answered a question from the teacher. A student near the back whispered something, and two children laughed.

Layla's face became hot. Her stomach tightened. For a moment, she forgot the next sentence on her note card.

She thought:

"They think I am stupid. I should never speak in class again."

What should Layla check before accepting that thought?

Which response best follows Notice — Name It to Tame It — Check — Choose?

Layla quietly named the feeling: "I feel embarrassed and nervous."

She checked the thought. She did not actually know why the children laughed, and one difficult moment did not prove that she was bad at speaking.

She took one breath, looked at her note card, and finished the next sentence.

After class, she calmly asked the teacher what had happened. The teacher explained that the students had laughed at a picture that appeared on another student's screen, not at Layla's answer.

Layla learned that a feeling can be real even when the first interpretation is wrong.

7. My One-Minute Feeling Check

Use the four steps once before the next lesson:

  1. Notice: What happened in my body?
  2. Name It to Tame It: What was I feeling?
  3. Check: What was the feeling signaling? What thought or urge came with it?
  4. Choose: What did I do that was safer, wiser, or more pleasing to Allah?

Tell one trusted adult what happened. No long journal is required.

8. Parent / Teacher Guide

Avoid saying

  • "Stop feeling that."
  • "You are overreacting."
  • "Good Muslims do not feel this."
  • "There is nothing to be upset about."

Say instead

  • "I can see this feeling is strong."
  • "Let us name it."
  • "What might it be signaling?"
  • "Is the thought certainly true?"
  • "Now let us choose the right action."
